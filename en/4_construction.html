Building Works Safety

Precast Concrete Components and Modular Integrated Construction Advantages

The shift from traditional cast-in-situ concrete construction to the use of Precast Concrete Components (PCC) and Modular Integrated Construction (MiC 1.0 and MiC 2.0) significantly enhances site safety and creates a healthier working environment for construction personnel. By adopting these advanced construction methods, the need for extensive formwork and temporary propping on site is eliminated, reducing risks associated with working at height, manual handling, and site congestion. Precast and modular components are manufactured in controlled factory settings, ensuring higher quality, better hygiene, and fewer on-site hazards. This transformation not only improves safety for workers but also supports sustainable construction practices by minimising waste and site disturbances.

The adoption of precast components-such as precast façades, staircases, semi-precast slabs, beams, parapets, and water tanks has greatly improved site safety by reducing the need for formwork and temporary propping, especially at height. To further protect workers from fall hazards and strenuous site work, volumetric precast bathrooms and kitchens have been introduced, allowing much of the complex installation to be completed in controlled factory environments. The latest advancement, MiC 2.0, features six-face modules that are self-supporting and require no temporary works inside the module, further minimising on-site risks. By shifting more construction activities off-site and into safer, weather-protected factory settings, these innovations significantly enhance the safety, health, and well-being of workers while also supporting cleaner, more sustainable construction practices.

Lifting Operations

Lifting operations are a major activity during construction and require careful planning to protect workers and site personnel. Early identification and clear demarcation of lifting zones during the site planning stage are essential to prevent collisions and ensure safe movement of loads. This is particularly important for sites located in urban areas or those with multiple cranes, where the risk of interference with adjacent sites or nearby tower cranes is higher. To further safeguard workers, all tower cranes are now equipped with advanced anti-collision systems that continuously monitor crane movements and provide real-time alerts, effectively minimising the risk of accidents.
To enhance lifting safety and prevent accidents, it is mandatory to use a double chain sling when lifting hoppers. This precaution ensures that if one chain fails, the other will securely support the load, significantly reducing the risk of dropped loads and protecting workers from potential injury.

Height Work Protection

To prevent falls from height and protect workers, all elevated work areas such as typical floors and building edges must be equipped with sturdy working platforms featuring adequate guardrails and toe boards. Additionally, these platforms should be kept clear of debris and materials to maintain a safe and unobstructed workspace, minimising trip hazards and ensuring a secure environment for personnel working at height.

Rebar Protection

Slips, trips, and falls on the same level are significant hazards on construction sites, particularly due to unprotected protruding steel bars. To safeguard workers, it is essential to provide proper protective measures such as rebar caps, wooden troughs, or steel planks over exposed steel bars. These precautions help prevent serious injuries in the event of a fall and contribute to a safer working environment for all personnel.

Safety Management Systems

Contractors are required not only to comply with all applicable safety regulations but also to strictly adhere to the specific safety requirements outlined in the contract specifications throughout the construction process. To ensure the protection of all workers and personnel on site, contractors must prepare and submit comprehensive safety documentation, including a detailed Safety Plan, safe working procedures, and risk assessments for all high-risk activities. These documents must clearly demonstrate that construction activities will be conducted in a manner that prioritises worker safety and minimises hazards.

Safety Plan

Safety Plan should encompass the company’s safety and health policies, organisational responsibilities, safety committee, training programmes, job hazard analysis, control measures, and emergency response protocols. Regular review and updating of these safety documents are essential to adapt to evolving site conditions and maintain effective risk control. By fulfilling these contractual safety obligations, contractors help create a safer work environment, reduce the likelihood of accidents, and ensure compliance with both regulatory and project-specific safety standards.

Housing Authority Safety Auditing System (HASAS) and Housing Authority Lift and Escalator Safety Auditing System (HALENSAS)

Safety Audits have been incorporated as a contract requirement in Building and Engineering contracts of Housing Department since 1996. The HASAS and the HALESAS establish a robust and independent framework to evaluate the effectiveness of contractors’ safety plans and their implementation on site. These systems are designed to promote consistent safety standards and heighten safety awareness among frontline supervisors and workers, ensuring that all personnel operate under the same high level of safety expectations. Safety audits are conducted quarterly by accredited safety auditors who rigorously assess compliance against key criteria covering safety management systems and site practices. Audit findings are meticulously recorded and communicated to contractors, serving as a vital tool to motivate continuous improvement, particularly in the practical application of safety plans and overall site safety performance. By fostering a culture of accountability and proactive risk control, HASAS and HALENSAS significantly contribute to protecting workers from occupational hazards and reducing workplace incidents throughout construction and lift/escalator installation works. The HASAS and HALESAS have been developed with a view to strengthen safety performance monitoring but not intended to relieve contractors and any responsible parties from their obligations to ensure site safety.

Surprise Safety Inspection Programme (SSIP) The SSIP is designed to enhance worker safety by promoting heightened vigilance and strengthening safety management through surprise site inspections conducted by independent safety inspectors. Its primary objectives are to elevate occupational safety and health standards on Capital Works New Works Contracts, reinforce third-party oversight of contractors’ safety performance-especially regarding the implementation of safety controls-and to emphasise the fundamental principle of “Safety First” as an essential component of quality construction. During each surprise visit, inspectors assess high-risk activities and physical site conditions against stringent compliance criteria, immediately addressing any unsafe acts or conditions to protect workers. The inspection process includes direct engagement with site personnel and contractors to foster open communication, ensuring that safety issues are promptly identified, documented, and rectified. By integrating SSIP scores into contractor performance assessments and incentive schemes, the programme motivates continuous improvement in site safety practices, ultimately safeguarding the health and well-being of all workers on construction sites.

Smart Site Safety System Application In reviewing the construction accidents occurred in new works in recent years, 4S is readily implemented for monitoring high-risk construction activities continuously. This can help uplift the site safety performance through early identification of potential dangerous incidents or dangers and taking immediate follow- up mitigation actions. The Smart Site Safety System (4S), introduced in 2024 for contracts exceeding HK$30 million, represents a significant advancement in safeguarding workers and site personnel through the integration of cutting-edge technologies. By leveraging virtual reality for immersive safety training, 4S enhances workers’ hazard awareness and emergency preparedness before they step onto the site. The Centralised Management Platform (CMP) and electronic permit-to-work system streamline the approval process for high-risk activities-such as crane operations, hot works, and working at height ensuring only authorised and competent personnel are involved. Real-time authentication of equipment operators, access control to hazardous zones, and automated alerts for unsafe acts or conditions-powered by artificial intelligence and IoT sensors-provide immediate warnings to workers, enabling them to take corrective actions before incidents occur. The system also tracks the location and status of personnel, plants, and equipment, and deploys smart monitoring devices to continuously assess workers’ health and safety. For confined spaces, dedicated monitoring further protects against hidden dangers. By adopting 4S, frontline workers benefit from early detection of potential hazards, fostering a proactive safety culture that helps prevent accidents and ensures a safer, healthier construction environment for everyone on site.

Lift Installation Safety

When performing lift installation works, strict safety measures must be implemented to protect workers and personnel from hazards associated with unprotected lift well openings and related activities which area protection of lift well openings, safety nets inside lift shafts, permit-to-work system, use of metal scaffolding and alternative and work restrictions and communication.
For protection of lift well openings, install four-leaf metal doors at lift well openings to prevent falls of persons and falling objects. These doors should be lockable from the outside but accessible from inside without the need for a key, ensuring emergency egress and security simultaneously.
Install safety nets with mesh sizes smaller than 20mm x 20mm inside the lift shaft at intervals not exceeding 20 meters. These nets serve as barriers to catch falling objects and protect workers below from injury.

Contractors must implement a permit-to-work system for hazardous trade processes related to lift installation. This ensures that all high-risk activities are authorised, monitored, and supervised closely to enforce safety precautions.
Metal scaffolding is preferred for lift shaft works due to its superior strength, fire resistance, and reliability. However, where site conditions permit, a guided suspended working platform should be adopted as a safer alternative, providing better stability and reducing risks.
Prohibit simultaneous work at different levels within the lift shaft to minimise the risk of accidents and falling objects.

Provide adequate and effective communication tools, such as walkie-talkies, for workers operating inside the lift well to maintain constant contact and enhance coordination and emergency response.
These safety measures collectively reduce the risk of falls, injuries from falling objects, and other hazards associated with lift installation work, ensuring a safer working environment for all personnel involved.

Electrical Safety Protocol To ensure the safety of workers and personnel during electricity supply system installation and maintenance, the following safety measures must be strictly observed.
All electrical cables at elevated locations must be firmly fixed and secured to prevent damage that could lead to electrical hazards such as electric shock. All circuits and exposed conductive parts must be properly earthed. Earth leakage protection devices should be installed on sockets supplying portable equipment to quickly detect and interrupt fault currents, reducing the risk of electric shock. Utilize a 110-volt power supply for portable electrical equipment where possible to minimise the severity of electric shocks.

Display clear warning labels such as ‘Electric Hazard’ on main electrical switches and post first aid instructions for electric shock prominently in plant rooms to increase awareness and preparedness.
Before any electrical work, isolate the power source using lockout and tagout procedures. This involves locking the isolator in the ‘off’ position with a unique lock and placing a tag warning that the equipment must not be energised until the lock is removed. This prevents accidental energisation and protects workers from electrical hazards.

Equip MCBs with lockable devices that allow the circuit to be securely isolated during maintenance. These assistive devices should be readily available in electric meter rooms and provided to responsible personnel. For domestic units, such devices should be provided to flat owners or made available for borrowing by tenants to ensure safe isolation of electrical circuits.

Conduct periodic inspections to identify and rectify insulation wear, circuit overloads, loose connections, and damaged cables. Remove defective equipment from service immediately to prevent accidents.

Workers must wear insulating gloves, flame-resistant clothing, safety goggles or face shields, and insulated footwear to protect against electrical shocks, arc flashes, and burns during electrical work. Avoid working near live electrical parts without proper isolation. Keep electrical equipment away from water sources to prevent shock hazards. Use non-conductive tools and working platform when working near power lines.
Ensure workers are trained on electrical hazards, safe work procedures, and emergency response. Maintain clear communication among personnel during electrical works to coordinate safety measures effectively.

Safety-Optimized Practices in Civil Engineering The use of precast concrete manholes in drainage works significantly enhances worker safety by allowing manhole structures to be fabricated offsite, thereby minimising the time workers spend in open pits and reducing their exposure to hazards such as falling objects and potential soil collapse. Additionally, adopting trenchless excavation methods like slurry micro-tunneling further improves safety for both workers and the public. This technique uses remotely controlled boring machines to install underground drains and utilities without the need for extensive open-cut trenches, which not only minimises surface disruption and traffic interruptions but also reduces the risk of ground settlement and accidental cave-ins. Together, these modern construction methods are effective in mitigating traditional risks associated with civil engineering drainage works, fostering a safer working environment for all personnel involved.

Safety Requirement for Excavation Works Prior to the commencement of road works, comprehensive Temporary Traffic Arrangements (TTAs) must be established to minimize the risk of traffic accidents that could result in serious injuries or fatalities. These TTAs should be carefully planned in consultation with relevant government departments, approved by the appropriate authorities, and closely monitored during implementation. To further enhance safety, contractors must collaborate with clients to implement comprehensive safety measures, creating a secure working environment for workers and the public.
Key safety practices include the erection of clear publicity boards displaying the project proponent, construction duration, and contact information, as well as the display of excavation permits and TTA plans for inspection. Continuous barriers should be installed to fence off work zones, accompanied by road hazard warning lanterns to alert drivers and pedestrians. The name and contact details of responsible personnel should be prominently displayed, and all workers and visitors must wear appropriate personal protective equipment.
During excavation works, adequate trench support must be provided for trenches deeper than 1.2 meters, and all trenches or holes exceeding 2 meters in depth should be properly guarded according to safety regulations. Where necessary, trenches and holes should be securely covered with steel plates or decking, and safe access routes, such as staircases with handrails, should be provided. Any ground settlement or damage to nearby roads, buildings, slopes, or public utilities should be closely monitored, and immediate action must be taken if instability is detected.
Before excavation works begin, underground utilities detection must be conducted to prevent worker injuries and avoid damage to existing utilities. This process adheres to all relevant regulations and codes of practice, utilising the latest utility records and drawings, and engaging competent personnel to use suitable underground utility detection devices, such as pipe and cable locators or ground-penetrating radar to determine the routes of underground utilities. In critical areas, trial pits may be used to confirm the exact locations of buried services. All works near power cables and gas pipelines must follow the steps outlined in the codes of practice issued by the relevant authorities.
To further safeguard the integrity of the road and surrounding infrastructure, settlement monitoring should be implemented during excavation and tunneling works. Settlement monitoring points should be established according to the monitoring plan, and if ground settlement exceeds predicted values, work should be halted immediately for investigation and remedial action.
By strictly enforcing these safety measures and maintaining vigilant supervision, the risks associated with road works can be significantly minimised, ensuring the safety and well-being of workers, road users, and the public.

Managing Risks and Safety in Arboricultural Works Hazards and risks are inherent in all arboricultural works without exception. Arboricultural treatment includes tree pruning, removal, transplanting, guying and other establishment works conducted at ground or elevated level, often involve the use of machinery, lifting, and working at heights. Therefore, it is essential to maintain a safe system of work throughout all work procedures to minimise potential risks to both workers and the public.

Arboricultural works

Workers participating in tree work operations must have relevant training and experiences. Specific personnel shall be deployed to conduct specific types of works, such as tree climber and chainsaw operator, and to be supervised by a competent personal, for instance, a Tree Work Supervisor, to ensure that the work is carried out in accordance with good arboricultural practice and adequate occupational safety and health measures, including procedures outlined in the safe system of work.
When work is conducted at height, appropriate access equipment such as elevating work platforms, podium steps, or ladders should be utilized as the safe method for access and support. Aerial tree work by climbing should only be undertaken when necessary, and it must be carried out by a qualified Tree Climber under the supervision of a Tree Work Supervisor.
When tree work is performed at public area, the Tree Risk Assessors should conduct risk assessment on the potential hazards, evaluate the risks and formulate the most appropriate and safe method to carry out the tree work. Appropriate safety measures, such as provision of warning signs, cordoning off the work area with temporary fencing, and arranging workers to control pedestrian traffic, should be adopted when there is a potential risk affecting the public.

To avoid hazards during maintenance, underground services such as power conduits and manholes should be carefully located away from planter areas, and tree planting in confined spaces like small pits should be avoided in favour of soil corridors wherever feasible.
