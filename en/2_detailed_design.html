Main Entrance Gateset Design

For the main entrance gateset design, a lighter gateset is adopted to facilitate easier operation and to prevent falling hazards, thereby enhancing safety for workers and personnel involved in architectural work. Key safety features include the use of an offset pivot hinge floor spring, which ensures smooth and controlled gate movement, and the addition of inspection panels for easy maintenance checks. High-quality stainless steel fasteners such as screws, bolts, locknuts, thread-locking adhesive, spring washers, and safety pins are specified to secure all components reliably. The gateset height is reduced to minimise risks associated with handling and operation. Furthermore, a door lock status indicating light and clear signage are incorporated to provide immediate visual feedback on the gate’s locked condition, improving situational awareness. To prevent accidental gate detachment or collapse, stainless steel chains firmly fasten the door leaves to the frame, adding an extra layer of security.

Electrical and Mechanical Room Protection

To minimise the risk of water leakage compromising critical electrical and mechanical equipment, careful consideration must be given to the location of electrical and mechanical rooms within a building. Wet environments such as water tanks and booster pump rooms should not be situated above essential electrical service areas including lift machine rooms, photovoltaic equipment rooms, and emergency generator rooms to prevent the possibility of water ingress and subsequent electrical hazards. Where such an arrangement is unavoidable, additional protective measures such as a double ceiling slab or effective waterproofing with proper drainage should be provided between service rooms. Similarly, electrical service rooms and cable ducts should be located away from pipe ducts containing wet services, such as fire service pipes, water pipes, and water tanks. If separation is not practicable, waterproofing must be applied to the partition walls between these rooms to act as a barrier against potential leaks.

To prevent electrical power disruptions caused by water leakage or flooding from burst pipes in water pipe ducts or water meter rooms, it is essential to separate rooms designated for electrical services and water services as much as possible, either horizontally or vertically. This spatial separation significantly reduces the risk of water infiltrating electrical areas and causing hazardous situations for both building occupants and maintenance personnel. Where complete separation is not practicable due to site constraints, additional protective measures such as robust waterproofing and drainage systems should be implemented to further safeguard electrical installations.

Twin Tanks Water Supply

To ensure a continuous supply of fresh and flushing water during routine maintenance or cleaning, a twin tank water supply arrangement is recommended. This design allows one tank to remain operational while the other is being serviced, eliminating the need for complete water shutdowns and thereby minimising disruption to residents. From a safety perspective, the twin tank system reduces the risk of waterborne diseases such as Legionnaire’s disease by facilitating regular cleaning and maintenance without compromising water availability. For workers and maintenance personnel, this arrangement provides a safer working environment, as maintenance tasks can be carried out without pressure to rush or bypass safety procedures due to urgent water supply demands.

Structural Safety Innovations
Precast Concrete Components and Modular Integrated Construction

he adoption of Precast Concrete Components and Modular Integrated Construction (MiC) significantly enhances safety for workers and personnel by streamlining the construction process and minimising on-site risks. By shifting much of the construction work to a controlled factory environment, MiC reduces the need for high- risk activities such as working at height, manual handling of heavy materials, and exposure to adverse weather conditions. This approach results in better quality control, less construction waste, and reduced disturbance to both workers and the surrounding community. Recent enhancements in MiC design, such as the transition from side fixing to top fixing, provide a safe, permanent working floor for tasks like rebar fixing, eliminating the need for temporary elevated platforms that can pose fall hazards. Additionally, upgrading modules from five to six faces increases structural stability, reducing the requirement for temporary works inside the modules and further safeguarding workers during installation.

Flushing Water Safety

Clogging of dirt or grit in flushing water pipes can cause a rise in pressure, potentially leading to pipe bursting and creating serious safety hazards for personnel. To mitigate these risks, bucket-type strainers are installed at both the incoming flushing water pipe and the discharge side of the flushing water feed pump. This helps prevent debris from entering the system, thereby minimising the chances of flooding and reducing the need for manual repair work. Additionally, machine guards are provided to cover the moving parts of water pumps, protecting workers from accidental contact and injury during operation and maintenance.

Road Safety Enhancements

To enhance safety of workers and road users in civil engineering works, we have implemented a series of comprehensive design measures. At road junctions and refuge islands, non-illuminated retro-reflective traffic bollards are equipped with highly visible reflective sign plates. These plates are easily illuminated by vehicle headlights, especially at night or under low-light conditions, significantly enhancing visibility and road safety.

Tree Selection Matrix

In the detailed design stage of public housing landscape works, the principle of “Right Tree, Right Place” is rigorously applied to minimise safety risks for both workers and residents. Careful selection of tree species, with reference to the findings from micro-climate and ecological studies, ensures that only those suited to the specific site conditions such as local climate, soil, and exposure are planted. Trees chosen for podiums and slopes are matched in size, height, crown spread, and maturity to the physical constraints of the area, reducing the need and risk for intensive horticultural work later. Species with proven resilience characteristics such as shade, drought, wind, soil compaction, and pollution are prioritised according to the site constrains, lowering the frequency of plant replacement and maintenance, and thus reducing workers’ exposure to risks in horticultural works.

In areas prone to strong winds, secure guying and supports for trees are provided, with removable eye bolts installed along planter walls for safe anchorage, especially during the establishment stage of new trees. The design process uses micro-climate data to avoid tree planting in areas with excessive wind exposure whenever possible, further safeguarding maintenance personnel from falling branches or unstable trees.

Additionally, to protect both workers and residents, tree, shrub, and climber species with pointed leaves, toxic parts, or those likely to exacerbate allergies are avoided. This reduces the risk of accidental injury or health issues during planting, maintenance, and routine care.

Play Area Safety

In order to provide a safe and enjoyable play experience for users in communal play areas, careful attention is given to the design, installation, and maintenance of the play and fitness equipment that compiles with safety standards. Equipment is strategically installed to maximise play value while minimising hazards, with layout designs promoting a safe flow of movement and providing sufficient space to prevent collisions, particularly for users with disabilities. All play and fitness equipment must be inspected and endorsed by an Approved Playground Safety Inspector to confirm compliance with relevant safety standards and required safety margins.

Sufficient seating facilities under shade are provided near play areas for the caregivers, enhancing child safety supervision, offering shelter from direct sunlight during rest, and encouraging social interaction for users of all ages. Bilingual warning signs are installed at prominent locations, outlining the safety precautions and instructions for equipment users, including the suitable age range of use and the age range of children using the equipment that require adult supervision.

All communal play areas are equipped with Impact Absorbing Safety Materials (IASM) as a crucial safety measure to protect users from hazards, such as falls from height. The IASM layout design covers the necessary safety margins and use zones for each piece of play equipment, and the materials will be tested by a recognized laboratory in compliance with safety standards.
